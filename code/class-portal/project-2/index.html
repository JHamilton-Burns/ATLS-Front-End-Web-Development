A POCKET GUIDE TO:

Alpha-gal Syndrome

Mammalian Meat Allergy

What is it?

An allergy involving an IgE antibody response to galactose-Î±-1,3-galactose. This sugar, commonly known as alpha-gal, is found in all mammals except for Old World monkeys, apes, and humans, as well as some other organisms. The onset of AGS is associated with tick bites and was first discovered in 2009. Learn more

How do you get it?

In the United States, the Lone Star Tick is thought to be the primary cause of AGS. Repeated tick bites are associated with a rise in alpha-gal IgE. Learn more

Triggers

  • Mammals: beef, pork, lamb, bison, venison, goat, horse, rabbit, squirrel, kangaroo, antelope, buffalo, guinea pig, etc.
  • Lard
  • Dairy *

    Reactions

  • AGS reactions typically occur 3-8 hours after eating.
  • In up to 20% of cases, GI symptoms in isolation of other symptoms
  • Reactions to airborne alpha-gal, especially fumes from cooking meat
  • Joint pain/arthritis
  • Chronic pruritus [itchy skin]
  • Anaphylactic reactions in up to 60% of cases
  • Chronic spontaneous urticaria [hives]

    • Why all the fuss?

    AGS is already a common allergy in some regions of the world, including the U.S., where the number of diagnosed cases has risen from 12 in 2009 to over 34,000 in 2019

    Roughly 60% of people with AGS have anaphylactic reactions and 30-40% have cardiac and respiratory symptoms.

    In one recent study of anaphylaxis, AGS was found to be the number one trigger, accounting for 33% of cases with a definitive cause. The number two cause was all other food allergies combined at 28%.

    AGS is the leading cause of adult-onset food allergy and anaphylaxis throughout the South and much of the eastern U.S.
